= Glossary Quality Criteria and Key Term Audit
:toc:
:toclevels: 3

== Purpose

This Lecture Topic Task defines quality criteria for glossary terms and applies those criteria to key terminology used in the Home Inventory project. The goal is to improve documentation consistency, reduce ambiguity, and support clearer communication across domain analysis, requirements, design, implementation, and verification work.

A glossary is useful only if its terms are clear enough for different teams to apply them consistently. In this project, terminology matters because the same ideas appear across multiple feature areas: core inventory, alerts and restock, sharing and budget, reports, authentication, and automation. If terms such as "inventory," "item," "alert," or "household member" are interpreted differently, later requirements and implementation decisions may become inconsistent.

This audit does not replace the existing terminology file. Instead, it evaluates selected terms from the existing terminology documentation and identifies where the current definitions are strong, where they may create ambiguity, and where small refinements would improve usefulness.

== Glossary Quality Criteria

The following criteria are used to evaluate whether a glossary term is strong enough to support project communication and later requirements work.

[cols="1,3,3",options="header"]
|===
| Criterion | Meaning | Why it matters

| QC-1: Clear definition
| The definition states what the term means in direct, understandable language.
| Reduces ambiguity and helps all teams use the term the same way.

| QC-2: Consistent with project terminology
| The term uses other official project terms instead of informal or conflicting synonyms.
| Prevents terminology drift across documentation sections.

| QC-3: Distinct from related terms
| The definition makes clear how the term differs from similar concepts.
| Prevents overlapping meanings, especially between domain, requirements, and implementation terms.

| QC-4: Useful for requirements and design decisions
| The term helps the team reason about system behavior, responsibilities, permissions, alerts, or data modeling.
| Ensures the glossary supports engineering decisions rather than only describing vocabulary.

| QC-5: Traceable to context
| The definition identifies whether the term belongs mainly to the domain, requirements, or implementation layer.
| Helps readers understand whether the term describes the real-world problem, the system-to-be, or a technical solution.
|===

== Audit Method

Each selected term is evaluated using three outcome labels:

* *Strong:* The current definition is clear and useful with no major change needed.
* *Needs minor refinement:* The term is mostly clear, but a small wording improvement would reduce ambiguity.
* *Needs clarification:* The definition may overlap with another term or leave an important interpretation open.

The audit focuses on 10 high-value terms because they are central to inventory tracking, sharing, alerts, and automation.

== Key Term Audit

=== 1. Household member

Current definition:

"A person who belongs to a household and participates in consuming, replenishing, or coordinating around items."

Audit result: *Strong*

[cols="1,3",options="header"]
|===
| Criterion | Evaluation

| QC-1: Clear definition
| Clear. The definition identifies the actor and the actor's role in the domain.

| QC-2: Consistent with project terminology
| Strong. It uses official terms such as household, consuming, replenishing, and coordinating.

| QC-3: Distinct from related terms
| Strong. The terminology file explicitly distinguishes "Household member" from "User," where "User" is system-facing.

| QC-4: Useful for requirements and design decisions
| Strong. This term supports permissions, shared inventory visibility, replenishment responsibility, and household coordination.

| QC-5: Traceable to context
| Strong. It is marked as a Domain term.
|===

Recommendation:

No major change needed. The definition is clear and useful. If the team wants even stronger distinction from "User," the notes could mention that a household member may or may not currently be logged into the system.

Suggested refinement:

"A person who belongs to a household and participates in consuming, replenishing, or coordinating around household items; in the system-to-be, a household member may correspond to a user account."

=== 2. Tracked inventory record

"A representation of availability used by household members (fridge note, list, memory, chat message). May diverge from reality."

Audit result: *Strong*

[cols="1,3",options="header"]
|===
| Criterion | Evaluation

| QC-1: Clear definition
| Clear. It states that this is a representation, not the physical inventory itself.

| QC-2: Consistent with project terminology
| Strong. It uses "availability" and "household members," both official glossary terms.

| QC-3: Distinct from related terms
| Strong. It clearly differs from "Inventory (reality)."

| QC-4: Useful for requirements and design decisions

| QC-5: Traceable to context
| Strong. It is marked as a Domain artifact.

Recommendation:

No major change needed. This is one of the strongest glossary entries because it captures an important domain distinction.

Suggested refinement:

None required. Optional wording could say "may be incomplete, outdated, or wrong" to make the divergence more explicit.

=== 5. Availability

Current definition:

"A household's current understanding of how much of an item is left, often qualitative ('full', 'almost empty', 'backup')."

Audit result: *Needs minor refinement*

[cols="1,3",options="header"]
|===
| Criterion | Evaluation

| QC-1: Clear definition
| Mostly clear. The phrase "current understanding" correctly signals that availability may be approximate.

| QC-2: Consistent with project terminology
| Strong. The definition aligns with the tracked-record-versus-reality distinction.

| QC-3: Distinct from related terms
| Moderate. It is related to Quantity, Low-stock judgment, and Availability State, so the distinction could be sharper.

| QC-4: Useful for requirements and design decisions
| Strong. This term supports low-stock logic, manual updates, and qualitative household behavior.

| QC-5: Traceable to context
| Strong. It is marked as a Domain property.
|===

Problem identified:

The term is useful, but it could be confused with exact quantity or with system-derived actionable states. Availability is the household's understanding of what remains; quantity is a precise or approximate measure; actionable state is a system-recognized condition such as LOW_STOCK or OUT_OF_STOCK.

Recommendation:

Clarify that availability can be qualitative or quantitative, and that it may be represented in the system as quantity or state later.

Suggested refinement:

"A household's current understanding of how much of an item is left. Availability may be qualitative, such as 'full' or 'almost empty,' or represented later as a quantity or actionable state in the system."

=== 6. Low-stock judgment

Current definition:

"A decision that an item is running low, based on a household strategy ('feels light', 'opened backup') or a numeric threshold. Not inherently numeric."

Audit result: *Strong*

[cols="1,3",options="header"]
|===
| Criterion | Evaluation

| QC-1: Clear definition
| Clear. The definition explains that low-stock can come from either human judgment or numeric threshold.

| QC-2: Consistent with project terminology
| Strong. It uses household strategy, backup, and threshold language consistently.

| QC-3: Distinct from related terms
| Strong. The note "Not inherently numeric" helps distinguish domain judgment from implementation-level threshold logic.

| QC-4: Useful for requirements and design decisions
| Strong. This term supports both manual and automated low-stock detection strategies.

| QC-5: Traceable to context

Recommendation:

No major change needed. This term is well-defined and helps prevent a common design mistake: assuming that all low-stock conditions must be numeric.

Suggested refinement:

=== 7. Alert / notification (domain-level)

Current definition:

"A message that informs a household member about an actionable condition (low-stock, out-of-stock, expiring, expired, budget threshold crossed). Delivery method is an implementation choice."

Audit result: *Needs minor refinement*

[cols="1,3",options="header"]
|===
| Criterion | Evaluation

| QC-1: Clear definition
| Clear. It identifies the purpose of the message.

| QC-2: Consistent with project terminology
| Strong. It uses household member and actionable condition.

| QC-3: Distinct from related terms
| Moderate. The distinction from Notification record and Push notification is present in the notes, but could be made more explicit.

| QC-4: Useful for requirements and design decisions
| Strong. The definition correctly separates the domain-level alert from implementation delivery.

| QC-5: Traceable to context
| Strong. It is marked as a Domain artifact/event.
|===

Problem identified:

The term combines "alert" and "notification," which is acceptable if the project treats them interchangeably at the domain level. However, later documents also define "Notification record," "Push notification," and "In-app notification list," so the definition should emphasize that this term refers to the domain message concept, not the stored database record or delivery mechanism.

Recommendation:

Keep the term, but sharpen its distinction from implementation-specific notification objects.

Suggested refinement:

"A domain-level message concept that informs a household member about an actionable condition, such as low-stock, out-of-stock, near-expiry, expired, or budget threshold crossed. It does not specify whether the message is stored in-app, pushed to a device, or delivered by another mechanism."

=== 9. Scheduled sweep

Current definition:

"A periodic background check that evaluates low-stock and expiration conditions without requiring the user to open the system."

Audit result: *Needs minor refinement*

[cols="1,3",options="header"]
|===
| Criterion | Evaluation

| QC-1: Clear definition
| Clear. The definition explains the basic function.

| QC-2: Consistent with project terminology
| Mostly strong. It uses low-stock and expiration conditions consistently.

| QC-3: Distinct from related terms
| Moderate. It could be more clearly distinguished from immediate transaction-based checks.

| QC-4: Useful for requirements and design decisions
| Strong. This term supports automation, observability, retries, and alert generation.

| QC-5: Traceable to context
| Strong. It is marked as a Requirements function.
|===

Problem identified:

The definition explains what the scheduled sweep is, but does not mention that some checks may happen immediately after transactions while scheduled sweeps handle periodic evaluation. This distinction matters because other project documents describe a hybrid timing model.

Recommendation:

Clarify that scheduled sweep is periodic and background-based, not the only possible evaluation mechanism.

Suggested refinement:

"A periodic background check that evaluates low-stock, near-expiry, and expiration conditions without requiring the user to open the system. It is distinct from immediate checks that may occur after a transaction or quantity change."

== Recommended Improvements

The following improvements are recommended for future terminology refinement.

=== Recommendation 1: Preserve the domain vs requirements vs implementation distinction

The current terminology file already tags each term by phase. This should be preserved because it helps prevent confusion between real-world concepts and system-specific mechanisms.

Examples:

* Domain-level: `Alert / notification (domain-level)`
* Requirements-level: `Notification record`
* Implementation-level: `Push notification` and `In-app notification list`

This distinction should continue to be enforced across project documentation.

=== Recommendation 2: Strengthen terms that sit between human behavior and system behavior

Terms such as `Availability`, `Low-stock judgment`, and `Scheduled sweep` are important because they connect real-world household behavior to system logic. These terms should explicitly state whether they describe:

* a household member's understanding,
* a system-computed state,
* a persisted record,
* or a background function.

This reduces the risk of treating approximate human judgment as exact system truth.

=== Recommendation 3: Avoid redefining glossary terms in feature-specific documents

Feature documents should use the official terminology instead of redefining terms locally. If a team needs a feature-specific interpretation, it should either:

* reference the official glossary term, or
* propose a refinement to `terminology.adoc`.

This prevents terminology drift across Core Inventory, Alerts & Restock, Sharing & Budget, and Reports & Insights.

=== Recommendation 4: Add examples only when they clarify scope

Examples are useful when they prevent ambiguity. For example, "Milk" as an item type and "a specific milk carton" as an item instance help clarify the distinction. However, examples should not replace the definition.

The strongest entries in the terminology file combine both:

* a general definition,
* and a concrete example.

== Implications for Milestone 3

This audit supports Milestone 3 by improving the reliability of the documentation base used for implementation and review. A consistent glossary helps teams write requirements, design workflows, and verify behavior using the same language.

The most important terminology distinction to preserve is:

* real-world household domain concepts,
* system-to-be requirements concepts,
* and implementation mechanisms.

Keeping these layers separate will reduce ambiguity as the project moves from documentation into more concrete implementation, validation, and verification work.

== Conclusion

The existing terminology documentation is generally strong. Most key terms are clear, well-scoped, and useful for later requirements and design decisions. The strongest entries are those that explicitly distinguish related concepts, such as `Item type` vs. `Item instance (package)` and `Inventory (reality)` vs. `Tracked inventory record`.

The main improvement needed is not a full rewrite of the glossary. Instead, the team should make small refinements to terms that bridge domain behavior and system behavior, especially `Availability`, `Alert / notification (domain-level)`, `Scheduled sweep`, and `Restock list / planner`.

These refinements would improve consistency while preserving the existing terminology structure.
