== Introduction

The project's A3 document primarily identifies direct users (students living alone or students who are presently living together as roommates) as the key stakeholders for the Digital Home Inventory project. However, as discussed in the Domain Engineering lecture slides, stakeholder identification should be broad and take into account non-obvious stakeholders that would include "individuals, groups, institutions, enterprises, organizations, associations" who have interest in or dependency on the domain.

This lecture topic task document was drafted to identify non-obvious stakeholders whose perspectives might otherwise be missed, ensuring our domain description and eventual system design account for all relevant viewpoints.

== Non-Obvious Stakeholders

=== 1. Former Roommate

|===
| Attribute | Description 

| Description & Relationship | A student who previously lived in the shared housing and left items behind. They may have moved out mid-semester, graduated, or transferred. Their items remain in the inventory (e.g., kitchen appliances, bulk food, cleaning supplies).

| Interest/Dependency | They have a vested interest in retrieving their property or ensuring it's not discarded without consent. They depend on accurate inventory tracking to know what items they left behind and their current status.

| Domain vs. Implementation | Domain Engineering: Yes, the concept of "orphaned items" and ownership transitions is fundamental to shared inventory. Implementation: Yes, users who move out should be able to clear the inventory of items they are taking with them and unlink from a home inventory that they will no longer partake in.

| Requirements/Insights | The system could support "item ownership", tracking items for their respective owners or buyers. Ownership transfer workflows should be considered (e.g., "claim item," "relinquish item"). There is a need for "abandoned item" detection and handling policies.
|===

=== 2. Future Roommate

|===
| Attribute | Description 

| Description & Relationship | A student who has signed a lease to move into the apartment in the coming weeks or months. They need to coordinate what household items are already present versus what they need to bring or purchase.

| Interest/Dependency | They need visibility into the existing inventory to avoid duplicate purchases before moving in. They depend on accurate, shareable inventory snapshots.

| Domain vs. Implementation | Domain Engineering: Yes, pre-occupancy planning is a legitimate phase of the inventory lifecycle. Implementation: Possibly view-only access before account activation.

| Requirements/Insights | Have a "share inventory snapshot" functionality (read-only, expiring access). Have pre-move-in planning view separated from active household management. Have the ability to claim items upon move-in or flag items they'll contribute.
|===

=== 3. Guests or Visitors

|===
| Attribute | Description 

| Description & Relationship | Friends, partners, or family members who visit the household temporarily. They may consume items (snacks, drinks, toiletries) during their stay without logging these consumptions.

| Interest/Dependency | Minimal direct interest, but their behavior affects inventory accuracy. The system depends on accounting for "inventory shrinkage" from external consumption.

| Domain vs. Implementation | Domain Engineering: Yes, consumption events can originate from non-users. Implementation: They should not have access to manage the home inventory, but they could possibly have a way to log usage as visitors (could be through a QR code).

| Requirements/Insights | System should anticipate "unexplained consumption" and provide reconciliation mechanisms. "Guest mode" or temporary period where consumption is expected but not tracked. Audit features that prompt "Did you have guests this weekend?" to explain discrepancies. Have guests manually log their consumption through a QR code that gives access to logging consumption but not to actually manage the home inventory.
|===

=== 4. Parents or Family

|===
| Attribute | Description 

| Description & Relationship | Family members who send care packages, shop for the student during visits, or contribute bulk items like Costco groceries to the household inventory.

| Interest/Dependency | They want to know what their student actually needs before sending packages or shopping. They depend on inventory visibility to avoid sending duplicates.

| Domain vs. Implementation | Domain Engineering: Yes, items can enter the system through external contributors. Implementation: Possibly have a limited "contributor view" or wishlist sharing. Could simply have a guest or visitor mode that allows viewing an inventory without necessarily having permission to modify it.

| Requirements/Insights | Have a "wishlist" or "needs" guest or visitor view that family can access without full system access. Have the ability to mark items as "contributed by [contributor's name]" for acknowledgment. Notify when the student is low on items family typically supplies.
|===

=== 5. Landlord or Property Manager

|===
| Attribute | Description 

| Description & Relationship | Owns the apartment and is responsible for appliances (refrigerator, dishwasher, etc.) and possibly initial supplies upon move-in. May have rules about certain items (e.g., no space heaters, specific cleaning products to avoid damage).

| Interest/Dependency | They need to ensure their property (appliances) are properly maintained and that tenants aren't storing prohibited or illegal items. They depend on inventory visibility for appliances and restricted items.

| Domain vs. Implementation | Domain Engineering: Yes, the domain includes items that belong to the landlord vs. tenant. Implementation: Possibly have a read-only view for landlord portal integration.

| Requirements/Insights | Distinguish between tenant-owned and landlord-owned items. Have "restricted item" flags for prohibited products. Have maintenance tracking for appliances (e.g., "fridge filter due for replacement").
|===

=== 6. Multiple Roommates Unknown to Each Other Assigned to Live Together

|===
| Attribute | Description 

| Description & Relationship | Students who are given or assigned housing that they will share with other students whom they haven't met yet or coordinated with. They need to plan who brings what to avoid ending up with three toasters and no coffee maker. Unlike friends choosing to live together, these students lack existing trust and communication channels, making coordination more challenging.

| Interest/Dependency | They need pre-occupancy coordination tools that work for groups of strangers. They depend on inventory planning features that facilitate fair contribution splitting without pre-existing relationships.

| Domain vs. Implementation | Domain Engineering: Yes, the "pre-household formation" phase exists. Implementation: Yes, planning mode separate from active household mode.

| Requirements/Insights | Have a "pre-household planning" workspace. Have a "who's bringing what" registry functionality. Have a migration path from planning workspace to active household inventory.
|===

== Perspective Statements

=== Phenomenon: Item Ownership

|===
| Stakeholder | Perspective 

| Former Roommate | "Ownership is about retrieving my property after I've moved out. I need to know what I left behind, its condition, and have a way to reclaim it."

| Landlord or Property Manager | "Ownership is about distinguishing what belongs to me (appliances, fixtures) versus what belongs to tenants. I need to ensure my property isn't damaged or removed."

| Multiple Roommates Unknown to Each Other Assigned to Live Together | "Ownership is about who contributes what. Even if we share something like a toaster or coffee maker, the one who brought it has a claim to it."
|===

=== Phenomenon: Inventory Accuracy

|===
| Stakeholder | Perspective 

| Future Roommate | "Accuracy means I can trust the system to tell me what's already in the household before I move in so I don't buy duplicates."

| Guests or Visitors | "Accuracy? I just ate a snack. I don't think about inventory at all, for it's not my house. The system's accuracy will suffer because of me unless I could perhaps have a way to log my consumption."

| Parents | "Accuracy means I can send exactly what my student needs instead of guessing or asking what they're low on."
|===

=== Phenomenon: Notification Timing

|===
| Stakeholder | Perspective 

| Future Roommate | "Notify me before I move in about what's already in the apartment so I don't buy duplicates during my move-in shopping."

| Parents or Family | "Notify me when you're running out of something so I can bring it."

| Multiple Roommates Unknown to Each Other Assigned to Live Together | "Notify me when someone has already bought or is bringing something so that I don't buy or bring duplicates."
|===

== Implications for System Design

Based on this stakeholder analysis, the following design considerations emerge:

1. **Flexible Ownership Model**: Support multiple ownership states (individual, shared, landlord, orphaned) with transfer workflows.

2. **Multi-phase Access**: Different system modes for pre-occupancy planning, active household management, and post-occupancy retrieval.

3. **External Contributor Views**: Limited-access views for parents, future roommates, and landlords without full system accounts.

4. **Shrinkage Reconciliation**: Features that account for consumption by non-users (guests) through periodic audits or guest mode logging.

5. **Abandoned Item Handling**: Policies and workflows for items left behind by former residents.
