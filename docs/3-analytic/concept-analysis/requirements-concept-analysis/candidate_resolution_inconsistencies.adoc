== Resolution Approach Classification

NOTE: In the following cases, user names (Mateo, Sofia, Julian, Andrea) refer to the personas defined in Section 4.2 (Personas).

Two primary strategies are used to resolve inconsistencies:

Elicitation-Based Resolution: relies on additional stakeholder input; clarifies intent, expectations, and real-world behavior

Formal Resolution Tactics: relies on structured reasoning and modeling techniques; resolves conflicts through consistency rules, prioritization, or abstraction

This distinction ensures that both human-centered and system-centered solutions are considered.



== Elicitation-Based Resolution Cases

=== Case EBR1: Clarifying Item Ownership Responsibility

Inconsistency:
Inconsistency between Andrea’s need for complete visibility and control over shared items (N4/N5) and Sofia’s expectation of autonomy for personal items, as reflected in IR-03 vs US-INV-02.

Resolution Approach:
Conduct stakeholder interviews and scenario walkthroughs to determine expected ownership and access behavior in shared households.

Outcome:

- ownership responsibility is explicitly assigned per household roles and items
- alternative shared responsibility models are evaluated and rejected based on stakeholder preference
- clear mapping between personas and requirement IDs is established for traceability

Rationale:
Direct stakeholder input ensures alignment with real-world expectations and usage patterns while balancing household coordination and individual autonomy.

---

=== Case EBR2: Notification Frequency Expectations

Inconsistency:
Conflict between Mateo’s desire for timely expiration alerts to avoid spoiled food (N2) and Julian’s preference for minimal alerts to prevent distraction during meal prep (N2/N3). This is reflected in DR-4 and the low-stock/expiration alert requirements.

Resolution Approach:
Elicit user preferences via surveys, scenario testing, and persona-based walkthroughs to determine optimal notification frequency.

Outcome:

- introduce configurable notification frequency settings per persona or household member
- default behavior is informed by common user expectations
- alerts are mapped to actionable states to minimize missed or redundant notifications

Rationale:
User feedback resolves ambiguity by grounding system behavior in actual preferences and personas, ensuring that notifications are helpful rather than intrusive.

---

=== Case EBR3: Definition of “Low Stock”

Inconsistency:
Disagreement between Andrea’s need for strict thresholds to maintain household stock (N4) and Sofia’s preference for flexible, item-specific thresholds to avoid overbuying (N5). Julian also requires precise thresholds for specialty dietary items (N5). Tied to DR-5 and US-INV-04.

Resolution Approach:
Engage stakeholders to define acceptable low-stock thresholds per item type or category, with flexibility to adapt to persona needs.

Outcome:

- establish user-defined thresholds per item or category
- allow flexibility for specialty items, balancing strict household management and individual preferences
- conflicts between personas are documented and resolved via consensus or configurable settings

Rationale:
Elicitation captures contextual differences that cannot be generalized effectively and aligns with system behavior with both household and individual needs.



== Formal Resolution Tactic Cases

=== Case FRT1: Priority-Based Requirement Resolution

Inconsistency:
Conflicting requirements between minimizing user effort and maintaining high data accuracy.

Resolution Approach:
Apply prioritization rules to determine which requirement takes precedence.

Outcome:

- prioritize reduced user effort for usability
- introduce supporting mechanisms (e.g., automation) to mitigate accuracy loss

Rationale:
Formal prioritization provides a structured way to resolve competing system goals.

---

=== Case FRT2: Constraint Enforcement for Data Consistency

Inconsistency:
Conflicting updates in shared inventory environments leading to inconsistent item states.

Resolution Approach:
Introduce domain constraints and validation rules to enforce consistent updates across shared inventory.

Outcome:

- enforce rules such as non-negative quantities and restricted simultaneous conflicting updates
- only authorized users for a specific `household_id` may modify shared items
- this resolution is implemented via Supabase Row Level Security (RLS) policies, ensuring that only authorized members of a household can modify stock levels

Rationale:
Formal constraints provide enforceable consistency rules independent of user behavior while leveraging Supabase’s access control capabilities.

---

=== Case FRT3: Abstraction of Item State Representation

Inconsistency:
Overly detailed or inconsistent representations of item states (e.g., partial usage, estimated quantities).

Resolution Approach:
Abstract item state into simplified, standardized representations.

Outcome:

- define discrete states (e.g., in stock, low, out of stock)
- reduce reliance on imprecise or highly variable data
- the simplified item states are broadcast to all roommates using Supabase Realtime, ensuring the UI reflects the abstract immediately across all devices without manual refreshes

Rationale:
Abstraction reduces ambiguity, improves model consistency, and leverages the technology stack to maintain synchronized states across all users.



== Comparative Analysis of Resolution Strategies

Elicitation-based approaches:

- align system behavior with real-world expectations
- reduce misunderstanding of stakeholder intent
- may require iterative validation

Formal tactics:

- ensure logical consistency and enforceable rules
- reduce ambiguity through structure and constraints
- may not fully capture human behavior nuances

Implications:

- both approaches are necessary for robust resolution
- elicitation informs correctness
- formal tactics ensure consistency and enforceability



== Implications for Concept Analysis

These candidate solutions demonstrate that resolving inconsistencies requires both:

- understanding stakeholder intent
- applying structured modeling techniques

The domain model must support:

- adaptability to refined requirements
- explicit representation of constraints and priorities
- integration of user-driven configuration where ambiguity exists

This combined approach strengthens the reliability and coherence of the Digital Home Inventory system.
