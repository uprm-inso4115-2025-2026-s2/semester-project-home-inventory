== Requirements Concept Analysis

This section analyzes requirement-level concepts derived from the domain rough sketch and relates them to the domain concepts established earlier in the document. Its purpose is to define the vocabulary and tradeoffs that later requirements must use consistently.

=== Starting Point: Requirement Drivers Anchored in the Rough Sketch

The rough sketch motivates the following requirement drivers:

* *Low-friction capture:* "If it takes more than a few seconds to log, I won't do it."
* *Shared coordination failures:* duplicate purchases, no clear replenishment responsibility, and reliance on fridge notes or chat.
* *Multiple storage locations:* the same item type may exist in more than one place.
* *Qualitative stock judgments:* households often use expressions such as "full", "almost empty", or "backup" instead of exact counts.
* *Expiration and waste:* food and medicine may expire unnoticed, and some items may exist in unopened duplicates.
* *Variable consumption:* exam weeks and guests can change usage rates.
* *Membership change:* households need a clear way to handle move-out and access removal.

These drivers shape the requirement-level concepts and tradeoffs below.

=== Actors and Goals

[cols="2,4",options="header"]
|===
|Actor (domain role) |Primary goals (outcomes)

|Student living independently (solo)
|Maintain a reliable understanding of what is available with minimal effort; avoid duplicate purchases; avoid unexpected shortages; reduce waste from expired items.

|Student living with roommates/cohabitants
|Maintain a shared understanding of availability; coordinate replenishment to avoid duplicate or missed purchases; distinguish shared vs personal items without conflict.

|Household member with budget-awareness focus
|Understand spending patterns for essentials; avoid overspending; plan purchases using summaries rather than memory.

|Household organizer
|Reduce confusion about where items are stored and how they are grouped; keep organization stable enough that others can follow it.

|Operations / monitoring stakeholder
|Ensure automated checks and alert generation are reliable, observable, and do not produce duplicates or noisy output.
|===

=== Requirement-Level Concepts

The following concepts are used to express later requirements without prematurely committing to a specific implementation.

[cols="2,4",options="header"]
|===
|Concept |Meaning in requirements

|Fast capture
|A tracking or update interaction that fits within a few seconds and does not require perfect detail at entry time.

|Tracked record vs reality
|The system represents availability, but real household inventory changes continuously. Divergence is expected and must be managed.

|Shared visibility
|Household members can converge on the same understanding of availability and recent changes.

|Actionable states
|States that directly support decisions, such as `LOW_STOCK`, `OUT_OF_STOCK`, `NEAR_EXPIRY`, and `EXPIRED`.

|State transition
|A change from one actionable state to another. Transitions are the main trigger for alerts in order to reduce repetition.

|Low-stock strategy
|The rule by which "low" is determined, such as a fixed threshold or reorder point.

|Near-expiry window
|The definition of "expiring soon", expressed as a time window before expiration.

|Scheduled sweep
|A periodic background check that evaluates low-stock and expiration conditions without requiring the household member to open the system.

|Notification record
|A persisted representation of an alert, including type, time, and read/unread status.

|Deduplication / anti-spam
|Rules that prevent repeated alerts or repeated list additions when nothing meaningful changed.

|Auto-add to restock list
|Rule-based conversion of actionable states into restock-list entries.

|Transaction history
|An append-only log of quantity changes with reason and timestamp, allowing the current tracked state to be explained.
|===

=== Conflicts and Tradeoffs

The requirement-level concepts above introduce several tensions that later requirements must resolve explicitly:

* *Speed vs accuracy* — requiring too much detail slows capture, but requiring too little may reduce trust in the tracked record.
* *Flexibility vs consistency* — household-specific practices are realistic, but too much freedom increases terminology drift and inconsistent usage.
* *Shared visibility vs privacy* — shared coordination improves when more information is visible, but some items and spending records remain personal.
* *Timely reminders vs notification fatigue* — reminders reduce shortages and waste, but repeated alerts reduce trust and may be disabled.
* *Simplicity vs “smartness”* — fixed thresholds and explainable triggers are robust, while predictive or highly adaptive behavior is harder to justify and verify.
* *Automation reliability vs user-device dependence* — background checks improve consistency, but they require clear observability, repeatability, and error handling.

These tradeoffs explain why later requirements must emphasize low-friction entry, explainable alerting, deduplication, shared-versus-personal separation, and traceable quantity history.

=== #AHP-Based Requirement Prioritization#

#This section documents an AHP-based method for requirement prioritization and provides a reproducible calculation path for the team.#

==== #Evaluation Criteria#

#The prioritization compares requirements using the following criteria:#

#* *User value* — how strongly the requirement supports waste reduction, coordination, or low-friction capture.
* *Feasibility* — how practical the requirement is under the current architecture and project constraints.
* *Impact* — how much the requirement improves overall household inventory behavior.
* *Implementation complexity* — how difficult the requirement is to build; lower complexity is preferred when value is similar.#

==== #Team Input Aggregation Method#

#Each team member supplies pairwise comparisons for the criteria and for the requirements under each criterion. The shared matrices are produced by averaging the corresponding matrix entries across team members.#

[source,r]
----
aggregate_matrix <- function(matrices) {
  Reduce("+", matrices) / length(matrices)
}
----

==== #AHP Process Steps#

#. Collect pairwise comparisons from all team members for the evaluation criteria.
. Aggregate the criteria matrices.
. Collect pairwise comparisons for the requirements under each criterion.
. Aggregate the requirement matrices for each criterion.
. Compute criterion weights and local requirement weights.
. Compute global scores from local weights and criterion weights.
. Rank requirements by final score.#
