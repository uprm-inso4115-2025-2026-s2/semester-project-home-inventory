1.2. Current Situation, Needs, and Ideas

1.2.1. Current Situation

Students living independently often lose track of household items such as groceries, cleaning supplies, toiletries, and sometimes medicine. Tracking is usually ad-hoc (memory, notes, receipts, or messages), so information becomes outdated quickly. As a result, students may buy duplicates, run out of essentials unexpectedly, and sometimes discover expired items during cleanup.

In shared households, the problem is worse: roommates lack visibility into what is already available, which increases duplicate purchases and makes budgeting harder. In many student households there is no consistent shared method for tracking items across people and time; tracking is ad-hoc (memory, notes, messages), so information becomes stale quickly.

1.2.1.1. Domain context

Students and young adults living independently often struggle to keep track of household items, shared supplies, and personal belongings. While several home inventory applications exist, most are designed for insurance documentation or business inventory management rather than lightweight everyday use.

As a result, many users rely on memory, notes, or informal tracking methods, which leads to forgotten purchases, duplicate buying, and poor visibility into shared household resources. Most tools students try (notes, basic lists, or heavier inventory apps) are either too informal to stay accurate over time or too cumbersome for everyday tracking, so shared inventory information quickly becomes unreliable.

1.2.1.2. Who is involved (actors)

  • Primary actors: Students and young adults managing personal household items

  • Secondary actors: Roommates or cohabitants sharing supplies

  • Organizations/systems involved: Existing home inventory mobile applications (e.g., Sortly, PantryCheck)

1.2.1.3. Current workflow (as-is)

  1. User purchases or owns household items

  2. User optionally records items manually in notes or an inventory app

  3. Inventory is rarely updated consistently over time

  4. User forgets what items are already available

  5. User repurchases duplicate or unnecessary items

  6. In shared households, roommates lack visibility into available supplies

1.2.1.5. Consequences

  • Time/cost impact: Users and students waste money on duplicate purchases and spend extra time checking inventory manually using written lists or other tracking software.

  • Quality/accuracy impact: When tracking relies on tools that are abandoned or partially used (e.g., because advanced features are unavailable or too cumbersome), the record becomes incomplete and therefore untrusted.

  • User experience impact: Frustration leads to abandonment of existing apps.

  • Organizational impact: Shared households experience coordination inefficiencies.

1.2.1.6. Assumptions and constraints (as-is)

  • Assumption: Target users prefer low-effort mobile workflows.

  • Assumption: Users are more likely to maintain inventory if entry is fast and simple.

  • Constraint: Users have limited patience for manual data entry.

  • Constraint: The solution must work well on mobile devices with intermittent connectivity.

1.2.3.1. Lightweight Smart Inventory

1.3. Scope, Span, and Synopsis

1.3.1. Scope and Span

Scope

The project’s scope is to carry out a software engineering project in the domain of student household management. The project will investigate how students living independently (alone or with roommates) keep track of household items and spending, and it will produce a system-to-be plus the supporting deliverables needed to build it responsibly (domain description, requirements, architecture/design, implementation, validation/verification, and documentation). The project targets common pain points caused by relying on memory and informal tracking, and it will support both individual and shared-household workflows. The project will encompass several key areas:

  • ⁠Domain Engineering: Analyze how students currently manage household items, including common breakdowns such as forgetting what is available, inconsistent updates, and poor roommate coordination. Identify the major actors (students living alone, students living with roommates/cohabitants) and the domain concepts (items, categories, locations/rooms, quantities, thresholds, expiration dates, shared responsibility).

  • ⁠Requirements Engineering: Define system requirements that support inventory accuracy, fast item updates, and coordination. Requirements will cover core inventory maintenance, item organization, information lookup before purchasing, shared-household coordination, and budget awareness, while also capturing constraints such as busy schedules, low tolerance for complex setup, and the need for privacy controls.

  • Software Architecture: Develop a high-level architecture that supports mobile use, persistent storage, and shared-household data synchronization. The architecture will identify major components and their responsibilities (client, data storage, synchronization, authentication/authorization, and notifications/reminders) and will be designed to support testability and maintainability.

  • Software Design: Design workflows that keep inventory maintenance low-friction (capture, update, lookup) and that remain understandable in shared households. Provide clear feedback when items are low, near-expiration, or shared, and present budget summaries in a way that supports planning rather than reactive purchasing.

  • ⁠Software Process: The project will follow an Agile, feature-team structure. Each feature team owns its feature end-to-end (research, design collaboration, implementation, testing, and documentation). Work will be tracked and reviewed using a structured issue and pull-request workflow to keep changes traceable and reviewed. Continuous feedback from team walkthroughs and scenario reviews will guide refinement of requirements and UX decisions.

Span

The project’s span focuses on typical student living environments (dorms, apartments, shared houses) where household management is informal and time-constrained. The initial span targets small households with a modest inventory and a small number of cohabitants, and it assumes frequent lightweight updates rather than occasional heavy maintenance.

  • ⁠Specifics of the System: Users can maintain a centralized inventory organized by room and category, update item quantities, and optionally track expiration dates and priorities. Household members can choose how they want to be informed about low-stock and upcoming expirations, like low-stock and upcoming expirations. Shared households can view a common inventory and distinguish between shared and personal items to reduce duplicates and conflict. Budget tracking connects purchases to categories and provides basic spending summaries and reports.

  • ⁠Target Audience and Expansion: The initial span targets students living independently, including those living with roommates. Expansion may include richer analytics (usage rates and trends), more advanced report types, optional integrations with external productivity or budgeting tools, and improved automation for restock planning. Any expansion will remain centered on student needs and low-friction maintenance.

  • ⁠Methodology and Maintenance: The project will proceed iteratively, refining documentation and implementation through validation and verification activities. Progress will be supported through iterative cycles of documentation, implementation, and validation/verification activities, refining scope and requirements as stakeholder feedback and early testing reveal gaps. The cycle of feedback and refinement will ensure the system remains practical for real student workflows and constraints.

1.3.2. Synopsis

This project addresses inefficiencies and stress in student household management that arise when tracking of essentials relies on memory or informal methods. The project will produce a system-to-be that supports lightweight tracking of household items and spending for students living alone or with roommates, with particular focus on minimizing maintenance effort, reducing duplicate purchases, and lowering the likelihood of items expiring unnoticed. Shared-household use will be supported so that cohabitants can coordinate around what is available and what needs replenishing.

Work will proceed through domain engineering and requirements engineering (including personas and scenarios), followed by architectural and design decisions and incremental implementation. Validation and verification activities (walkthroughs, scenario-based reviews, and testing) will be used to confirm that the evolving design matches stakeholder needs and that key behaviors work reliably. Across milestones, the team will iteratively refine the documentation and implementation based on evidence and feedback, aiming for an approach that remains practical for real student workflows over time.

1.4. Foundational Activities for System Development

A successful system-to-be is built on more than just code. The following activities establish the foundation upon which the Digital Home Inventory system will be developed, ensuring it accurately reflects student needs and performs reliably in real-world conditions.

1.4.1. Domain and Requirements Engineering

Learning how students actually live comes first. Through conversations and watching how people manage their daily routines—like using shared notes, talking things over with roommates, or simply trying to remember what they need—the project builds a picture of current habits that exist without any special application. Some students live alone, others share space with several people. Some organize carefully, others wing it. From these observations, the team will extract domain concepts (e.g., item types, locations, shared responsibility, replenishment routines, and expiration awareness) and translate them into stakeholder-centered needs, personas, and scenarios. These will then be refined into a first set of user stories and measurable requirements, with open questions explicitly recorded for later research. This reduces the risk of building requirements that do not match real student routines. To build this understanding, the team will use short interviews and guided walkthroughs of students’ current practices, collect a small set of concrete “day-in-the-life” scenarios, and review feedback from existing inventory/list tools to identify recurring friction points.

1.4.2. Documentation and Knowledge Management

As the project grows, decisions about the application should not live only in people’s heads or get buried in old messages. Writing things down in one place gives everyone access to the same information. Keeping documentation versioned and maintained alongside development work ensures it evolves with the system and remains easy to review and update. When someone joins later, they can read about why certain choices were made and how different pieces fit together. The same record helps keep everyone using the same words for the same things—whether "pantry" means only food or includes cleaning supplies, for example. Without this habit, people start remembering things differently or making assumptions that others do not share. The project becomes harder to keep consistent as new features get added over time.

1.4.3. Quality Assurance Planning

An application that surprises users or fails when they need it will not become part of anyone’s routine. Knowing when a feature is truly finished means agreeing on what success looks like beforehand. In addition to scenario-based validation, the team will plan verification activities such as unit/integration tests for core behaviors and periodic walkthroughs to check consistency between terminology, requirements, and implementation. For alerts, success might mean a student finds out about expiring yogurt in time to eat it. For the shared list, it might mean roommates see the same information when deciding who goes to the store. Walking through made-up but realistic situations with actual students helps catch mismatches between what the team builds and what users actually need. Talking through examples together—like a week in the life of someone trying to stick to a grocery budget—shows whether the planned features will hold up in real life. The point is an application that students find genuinely helpful, not one that simply passes technical checks.

1.4.4. Project Management and Coordination

Several groups working on different concerns of the same system-to-be need to stay connected. Regular conversations between teams working on inventory, alerts, budgeting, and reports help spot potential trouble before it slows everyone down. A shared work-tracking board will make ownership and progress visible and help the team manage dependencies between features. Looking over each other’s work before it becomes part of the main application catches small issues early. When the team working on reports needs information about spending, checking in with the budgeting team early saves redoing work later. These habits of talking and coordinating mean that when it comes time to bring everything together, the pieces fit without surprises.

1.5. Derived Goals

1.5.1. Primary Derived Goals

In addition to the primary goal already established by the current situation and needs, this project pursues several secondary outcomes that guide decision-making across milestones. A key derived goal is to encourage more sustainable household routines among students by reducing avoidable waste over time—such as items expiring unused or being repurchased unnecessarily—through better day-to-day awareness of what is already on hand. Another derived goal is to improve adoption and continued use: the project should support habits that remain realistic under busy student schedules, so the practice of keeping track of essentials does not collapse after an initial setup period.

A further derived goal is to strengthen coordination and trust in shared households. Even when people share expenses and supplies, misunderstandings about what is available and who will replenish items can create friction; the project aims to reduce these coordination breakdowns as a secondary social outcome of improved household awareness. Where budget awareness is in scope, an additional derived goal is to help students reflect on spending patterns around household essentials, supporting more informed planning rather than reactive purchases.

Finally, the project has internal quality goals that support sustainable progress across milestones. The team aims to maintain stable terminology and consistent documentation so decisions remain understandable and traceable over time, reducing rework and concept drift. The project will also emphasize evidence-based development by using validation and verification activities—such as personas, scenarios, walkthroughs, and testing—to continually check that the team’s understanding matches stakeholder reality and that key behaviors work as intended.

1.5.2. Competing Goals and Resolutions

Although the project’s derived goals reinforce one another in many ways, some of them also create natural tensions that must be handled explicitly. Identifying these tensions helps the team avoid inconsistent design decisions and produces derived requirements that clarify how the system should behave when important goals compete.

1.5.2.1. 1. Low-effort use vs. inventory accuracy and reliability

Competing goal A: Keep the system lightweight enough that students can realistically maintain it under busy schedules.
Competing goal B: Preserve inventory information with enough accuracy and completeness for reminders, shared coordination, and planning to remain trustworthy.

These goals compete because reducing friction often means reducing the amount of information required at entry time, but requiring too little information can make the inventory incomplete, stale, or unreliable. The project already emphasizes both low-effort maintenance and accurate inventory visibility, so the system must balance continued adoption with dependable data quality.

Resolution policy: The system should minimize required user effort during common workflows, but it must still collect and preserve the minimum information needed to support accurate inventory state and reliable system behavior.

Derived requirements:

  • DG-REQ-01 Users shall be able to create an initial item record with only the minimum information needed to identify and track that item, while adding richer metadata later as needed.

  • DG-REQ-02 Low-stock and expiration-related behaviors shall only occur when the item information required to evaluate those conditions is present and valid.

  • DG-REQ-03 Recorded item metadata shall remain stable and trustworthy over time unless an authorized user explicitly changes or removes it.

  • DG-REQ-04 Routine item updates shall remain lightweight enough to fit ordinary student workflows without creating excessive maintenance burden.

Trace hooks: N1, N2, N3; derived goal for adoption and continued use; reliability quality need; US-INV-01, US-INV-02.

1.5.2.2. 2. Timely reminders vs. avoiding notification fatigue

Competing goal A: Ensure users are reminded in time about low-stock and expiration conditions.
Competing goal B: Avoid excessive or poorly timed alerts that cause frustration, desensitization, or notification disablement.

These goals compete because a highly proactive reminder strategy may reduce the chance that important conditions are missed, but it also risks overwhelming users and undermining trust in the system. The project goals explicitly include automated low-stock reminders, expiration alerts, and customizable thresholds, while also emphasizing that notifications should remain helpful and relevant.

Resolution policy: The system should favor timely reminders for important conditions, but it must rate-limit and deduplicate recurring alerts so persistent states remain visible without becoming spam.

Derived requirements:

  • DG-REQ-05 Users shall receive low-stock reminders often enough to support timely replenishment, but not more than once per item for the same household member group within a single calendar day.

  • DG-REQ-06 Users shall receive expiration-related reminders often enough to support timely action, but not more than once per item for the same household member group within a single calendar day unless a new alert condition is created by a state change.

  • DG-REQ-07 Repeated sweep executions shall not produce duplicate notifications for the same item-condition pair within the same daily reminder period.

  • DG-REQ-08 Reminder behavior shall favor relevance and timeliness over notification volume by limiting prompts to conditions supported by valid item data and active alert rules.

Trace hooks: N2; usability and reliability quality needs; Lightweight Smart Inventory; US-ALT-01.

1.5.2.3. 3. Shared household visibility vs. privacy and least privilege

Competing goal A: Increase household coordination by making shared inventory visible to cohabitants.
Competing goal B: Protect user-specific or sensitive information by exposing only the information necessary for coordination.

These goals compete because coordination improves when household members can see what is available and what needs replenishment, but excessive visibility can expose ownership, spending, or personal-item information that not everyone should access. The project scope includes both shared inventory coordination and privacy/security needs, so the system must support collaboration without ignoring access boundaries.

Resolution policy: The system should expose the minimum shared information required for household coordination while restricting user-specific metadata to the appropriate scope and permissions.

Derived requirements:

  • DG-REQ-09 Household members shall be able to see the availability and replenishment-relevant status of shared items.

  • DG-REQ-10 Shared household data and personal or user-specific item data shall remain distinguishable when both exist in the same household context.

  • DG-REQ-11 Ownership, spending, and other user-specific item information shall only be visible to users with permission to view it.

  • DG-REQ-12 The distinction between shared items and personal items shall remain clear whenever that distinction affects replenishment or household coordination.

Trace hooks: N4, N5; derived goal for shared-household coordination and trust; security/privacy quality need; US-SB-01.

1.5.2.4. Summary of trade-off handling

These competing-goal resolutions clarify that the system must not optimize convenience, reminders, or transparency in isolation. Instead, the project balances adoption, data trustworthiness, alert usefulness, and household coordination by introducing derived requirements that are concrete enough to guide implementation and later validation and verification.

2.1.3. Narrative

A cohesive story of how the domain works, independent of any specific software system.

2.1.3.1. Overview

Students living independently go through a continuous cycle of acquiring, using, and replacing household items. They buy food and supplies when they have time and money, store them in locations within their living space, and consume them gradually. When items run low or run out, someone must notice and take action to replenish them. In shared households, this cycle involves multiple household members who must coordinate—or fail to coordinate—about what is needed, who will buy it, and when.

Tracking is typically informal. Many households rely on memory, a fridge note, a chat message, or a quick pantry check. This tracked understanding can diverge from reality: items may exist but be forgotten in a different location, backups may be opened without being recorded, and purchases may be duplicated because household members act on incomplete information. Waste can occur when items expire unnoticed or when duplicates are purchased while another instance is already available.

Consumption patterns are not stable. During exam weeks or when guests visit, items can be used faster than usual. Bulk purchases can increase the number of item instances in the household at once, sometimes with different expiration dates within a single bulk pack. The household cycle repeats constantly under constraints of limited time, attention, and coordination.

Some households also keep an informal understanding of spending on essentials. Purchases may be remembered loosely, estimated from receipts, or discussed among roommates when money feels tight. When spending is tracked (even imperfectly), it influences decisions such as buying in bulk for lower unit price, delaying non-urgent restocks, or choosing cheaper alternatives. In shared households, disagreements can arise when it is unclear which purchases are personal versus shared or when one member repeatedly pays for shared items. Budget awareness therefore becomes another dimension of household coordination, alongside item availability and replenishment.

2.1.3.2. Actors and artifacts
Actors (domain roles):

  • Students living alone (single-member household)

  • Students living with roommates/cohabitants (multi-member household)

  • Household members who often coordinate replenishment (informal “household manager” role)

  • Household members who focus on budget awareness (budget-conscious role)

Artifacts used today (domain-visible):

  • Fridge notes / whiteboards

  • Chat messages (e.g., group chat)

  • Notes app lists

  • Pantry photos

  • Receipts or memory-based estimates

2.1.3.3. Typical domain flow (as observed)

  1. Household members acquire items (sometimes in bulk) and store them in household locations.

  2. Items are consumed over time; availability decreases.

  3. A household member checks availability using informal artifacts (note, memory, pantry check).

  4. If the member judges an item to be low or out (often using qualitative strategies like “feels light” or “opened backup”), replenishment is triggered.

  5. In shared households, coordination may succeed (one person buys) or fail (duplicate buying).

  6. Some items expire before use; households may discover this late and discard them.

  7. Household members may keep informal awareness of spending; when tracked, spending influences replenishment choices and can create shared-vs-personal disputes.

  8. Membership changes (move-out) require the household to update who participates and who is informed.

2.1.6. Functions

Major domain functions/capabilities, described independently of UI. Each function includes a signature that names its inputs and outputs so that the effect of the operation can be stated precisely.

2.1.6.1. Stock item instance

Introduce new item instance of an item type into a household location, whether as a first stocking action or as replenishment. After stocking, checking the location will show that the given quantity of the item type has been added there.

#stockItem : Household × ItemType × Location × QuantityDelta × ExpirationDate? → Household#

  • Location determines where the new item instances are placed.

  • QuantityDelta is the number of units added.

  • ExpirationDate is optional; if provided, it is associated with the new item instances.

  • The returned Household reflects the updated inventory.

2.1.6.2. Check location

Inspect a location to determine which item types it contains and in what quantities. This is an observation function.

#checkLocation : Household × Location → List (ItemType × Quantity)#

  • Returns all item types stored at the given location along with their current counts.

  • This function enables expressing pre/post-conditions of other functions. For example, after calling stockItem with 3 units of rice at the kitchen cabinet, checkLocation for the kitchen cabinet will show the rice count increased by 3.

2.1.6.3. Move item instance

Change where an item instance is stored. After moving, checking the old location will no longer show this instance, and checking the new location will include it.

moveItemInstance : Household × ItemInstance × Location → Household

  • Location is the destination where the item instance will be placed.

2.1.6.4. Record consumption

Decrease availability of an item instance due to use.

#consume : Household × ItemInstance × QuantityDelta → Household#

  • QuantityDelta is the number of units consumed.

  • After consumption, the item instance’s quantity is reduced by the delta. If the new quantity reaches zero, the item is considered out-of-stock. If the new quantity falls at or below the low-stock threshold, the item is considered low-stock.

2.1.6.5. Judge low-stock

Determine whether an item type is considered low based on household strategy or threshold.

#isLowStock : ItemType × Availability × Threshold? → Bool#

  • If a Threshold is provided and Availability is at or below it, returns true.

  • If no threshold is set, the function may rely on qualitative strategies.

2.1.6.6. Determine expiration status

Classify an item instance’s expiration state when an expiration date exists.

#expirationStatus : ItemInstance × DateNow → (Active | NearExpiry | Expired | Unknown)#

  • Returns Unknown when the item instance has no expiration date or the date is unreadable.

  • NearExpiry applies when the item is within the near-expiry window.

2.1.6.7. Create restock entry

Add an item type to the restock list/planner with a reason.

#addToRestockList : RestockList × ItemType × Reason → RestockList#

  • Reason indicates why restocking is needed (LOW_STOCK, OUT_OF_STOCK, EXPIRED).

2.1.6.8. Defer / dismiss restock entry

Allow a household member to postpone or remove an entry from restock planning.

#deferRestock : RestockList × ItemType × Until? → RestockList#

  • Until is an optional date/time for when the entry should reappear.

2.1.6.9. Generate alert

  • AlertType is one of: LOW_STOCK, OUT_OF_STOCK, NEAR_EXPIRY, EXPIRED, BUDGET_THRESHOLD.

2.1.6.16. Domain Logic for Spending Tracking

Spending tracking in this domain represents how household purchases are recorded, attributed, and preserved over time. A spending event exists only when a financial transaction occurs within a household context, and it supports later budgeting, reporting, and accountability without depending on any specific implementation.

A spending event captures: * who paid * what was purchased * the monetary amount * when the transaction occurred * whether the cost is personal or shared

Core rules

  • Spending is recorded only when a purchase with a defined cost exists. Creating or editing non-financial item information does not by itself create spending.

  • Spending is always associated with a household context. It does not exist independently of the shared environment in which the purchase occurred.

  • Every spending event must identify a payer. Anonymous financial records are not allowed.

  • Spending must distinguish between personal and shared attribution. Shared attribution does not remove payer identification.

  • Spending records are historical and must remain traceable. Deleting or changing inventory items does not erase past spending.

  • Spending depends on a valid purchased item or recorded purchase context. Orphaned financial records are not valid.

  • If cost information is corrected, the correction must preserve traceability through a new entry or other explicit record of change rather than silently overwriting history.

Implications

These rules ensure that spending remains tied to household activity, preserves accountability, and can later support budgeting, reporting, and reimbursement logic without redefining the core domain concepts.

2.1.7. Behaviors

Recurring behaviors or rules in the domain: patterns, policies, and "how things are done."

2.1.7.1. Behavior patterns

  • Restock cycle: A household member consumes an item instance → availability decreases → low-stock is judged or quantity crosses a threshold → a replenishment decision is made → more of the item type is acquired.

  • Shared purchase flow: A household member buys a shared item → the purchase is recorded → the shared household understands that the cost belongs to the shared context rather than only one person.

  • Expiration monitoring: An item instance approaches its expiration date → the household notices or is informed → the item is used, discarded, or left in place depending on household practice.

2.1.7.2. Domain rules

  • One barcode corresponds to exactly one Item type definition.

  • A duplicate Item instance entry submitted within a short window may be treated as an addition to an existing tracked record rather than as a separate new record, unless household members explicitly keep them separate.

  • Spending entries are historical records; inventory corrections should not silently overwrite financial history.

  • A Household has one administrative responsibility at a time when that role is formally represented.

  • If that administrative responsibility is transferred, the new responsibility must be established before the previous admin leaves the household context.

2.1.7.3. Edge cases

  • If an item instance has no expiration date, expiration-based behavior cannot be derived from date information alone.

  • If a household admin attempts to leave without transferring that responsibility, shared-household administration becomes unresolved until another member takes over.

2.1.7.4. Shared-Household Invitation and Membership Changes

In shared households, participation may change over time as new members are invited, current members leave, or access is revoked. Invitation is therefore part of how household membership changes, even though the exact delivery and security mechanism belong to later requirements and implementation sections.

A household invitation is a temporary request that allows a participant to join a household context. At the domain level, the important states are:

  • sent

  • accepted

  • declined

  • expired

  • revoked

These states matter because they affect who participates in household coordination and who should continue to receive shared information.

2.1.8. Domain Invariants

This section states a small set of stable domain rules that must remain true across the household inventory domain.

2.1.8.1. Core invariants

  • A household has one or more household members while it is active.

  • An item instance belongs to exactly one household context.

  • An item instance is stored in some location, even if that location is forgotten or described informally.

  • A household may contain multiple item instances of the same item type at the same time.

  • A household member’s participation in a household may change over time.

  • Shared-household coordination depends on knowing which items are shared, which are personal, and who is allowed to view or manage them.

  • If spending is recorded, it remains associated with the household context in which it occurred.

  • If an item instance has an expiration date, it may become expired without being consumed.

2.1.8.2. Ownership and participation notes

Ownership and responsibility may vary by household practice. Some item instances are treated as personal, while others are treated as shared. For that reason, the domain must support both personal and shared responsibility without redefining the core concepts of household, household member, item type, and item instance.

Rather than introducing a separate set of competing ownership entities, the document keeps the shared-household context in the concepts of household membership, personal/shared item status, and household coordination.

2.2.2. Domain Requirements

Requirements expressed in domain language (capabilities/outcomes), independent of UI/technology. Terminology follows the domain description: Household, Household member, Location, Item type, Item instance, and Spending entry.

2.2.2.1. Inventory integrity

  • DR-1 (Barcode uniqueness): A single barcode shall map to exactly one Item type. The system shall not create a second item-type definition for an already-known barcode.

  • DR-2 (Item instance validity): Every Item instance shall reference an existing Item type at the time it is recorded.

  • DR-3 (Financial immutability): Removing an item from the tracked inventory (delete/archive/merge/split/adjust) shall not rewrite or delete associated Spending entries; financial history is immutable.

2.2.2.2. Shared-household rules

  • DR-4 (Household administration): Each Household shall have exactly one household admin role at a time. Administrative responsibility shall be transferable before the current admin leaves.

  • DR-4A (Invitation control): The system shall allow household participation to be extended through invitations governed by household permissions.

  • DR-4B (Invitation lifecycle): An invitation shall have a defined lifecycle state such as active, accepted, declined, expired, or revoked.

  • DR-4C (Revoked access): When a household member is removed from a household, their participation state shall change accordingly and access to that household’s shared information shall no longer remain active.

  • DR-5 (Membership independence): A system user may belong to multiple households, each with an independent membership role and membership state.

  • DR-6 (Attribution retention): Spending entries shall remain attributed to the household member who made the purchase, even when inventory quantities are reorganized (merge/split) or when items are marked shared/personal.

2.2.2.3. Duplicate handling and intent preservation

  • DR-7 (Short-window duplicates): If a duplicate item instance entry is detected within a short window (initially 30 seconds) with matching identity-relevant attributes, the tracked record shall aggregate quantity rather than create a separate entry (unless the user explicitly chooses to keep them separate).

  • DR-8 (Ambiguity guard): If item identity is ambiguous (near-duplicate name/brand/variant without authoritative identifier), the system shall not auto-merge without explicit confirmation.

  • DR-9 (Auditability of corrections): Inventory corrections (merge/split/adjust) shall be auditable and reversible at the record level; the system shall preserve what changed, when, and why.

2.2.2.4. Reporting integrity (if reporting is in scope)

  • DR-10 (Separation of concerns): Reports shall distinguish between inventory state (availability, locations, expiry) and spending history (amounts, dates, attribution); these shall not be conflated.

  • DR-11 (Budget derivation): Budget/spending summaries shall be derived from spending entries within a defined budget period and shall not be altered by non-financial inventory corrections (e.g., discarding expired items).

2.2.2.5. Alerts and restock (Milestone 1 baseline)

  • DR-12 (Threshold-driven low-stock): The system shall evaluate whether an item type is low-stock by comparing its current quantity to an item-specific threshold. When quantity falls at or below the threshold, the system shall flag the item type as low-stock and make this status visible to household members.

    Domain property behind this requirement: In the domain, low-stock is a judgment that an item is running low. This judgment may be qualitative or threshold-based. The requirement commits the system-to-be to threshold-based evaluation.

  • DR-13 (Expiration status): The system shall derive the expiration status of an item instance when an expiration date is present. If the expiration date is missing or unreadable, the system shall treat expiration status as unknown and shall not generate false-expired conditions.

  • DR-14 (Deduplicated alerting): The system shall deduplicate alerts and trigger them primarily on actionable state transitions, so that repeated checks without state change do not generate repeated alerts.

2.2.2.6. Duplicate prevention and data integrity

  • DR-15 (Normalization before comparison): The system shall normalize Item type definitions before duplicate comparison and before insert, so that duplicate detection produces consistent and reproducible results.

  • DR-16 (Spend attachment after duplicate resolution): The system shall finalize duplicate resolution for an item before creating any associated Spending entry, so that each spending entry is attached to exactly one resolved item type or household record without ambiguity.

  • DR-17 (Single item type per barcode at persistence): At persistence, one barcode shall map to exactly one Item type; the system shall enforce this constraint before insert.

2.2.2.7. Rationale / trace (Milestone 1)

  • DR-1..DR-3 support reliability of tracked inventory and financial records.

  • DR-4..DR-6 support shared-household accountability and coordination.

  • DR-7..DR-9 support data quality and conflict reduction during entry/corrections.

  • DR-10..DR-11 support clear reporting and budget reasoning.

  • DR-12..DR-14 support low-stock/expiry awareness with notification quality.

  • DR-15..DR-17 support duplicate prevention and data integrity.

The following edge-case interface requirements are recognized but deferred for detailed specification in later milestones. They are captured in this appendix, separate from core IRs, to avoid losing scope awareness and to support future traceability.

IR-EC-A: Near-Duplicate Variant Disambiguation

When a user adds an item whose name matches an existing item type but whose size, variant, or brand differs, the system must present the conflicting attribute highlighted and require the user to choose: "This is a different variant" or "Use existing item type." Same-name/different-size entries shall be treated as separate item type definitions by default and must not be auto-merged.

IR-EC-B: Stock Entry Separation by Expiration and Location

When a user attempts to merge two item instances for the same item type that have different expiration dates or different storage locations, the system must warn that merging will lose expiration/location tracking. The default action must be "Keep separate"; merging must require explicit confirmation.

IR-EC-C: Import Idempotency and Duplicate Spend Prevention

When a user uploads a CSV import file that was previously imported, the system must default to "Review before apply" and present a summary of potential duplicate transactions. Per-row, if a transaction fingerprint matches a previously imported record, the system must skip spend event creation by default and require an explicit "Import anyway" override.

IR-EC-D: Inventory Correction Without Budget Impact

When a user deletes or merges an inventory entry that has associated spend events, the system must present two independent controls: "Remove from inventory" and "Also mark purchase as mistake — exclude from budget". Inventory correction must never delete spend events automatically. Both actions must be logged separately in the audit trail.

IR-EC-E: Refund and Return Flow

When a user returns a purchased item, the system must provide a refund flow that: decrements inventory quantity, creates a negative spend entry linked to the original where possible, and requires amount refunded, date, and an optional link to the original receipt or spend event.

IR-EC-F: Waste and Shrinkage Adjustment

When a user removes expired, lost, or consumed items from inventory outside of a normal purchase/return flow, the system must: allow inventory adjustment without altering any spend event, require an adjustment reason, and offer an optional "Add to waste report" checkbox. Waste metrics must be reported separately from spending.

IR-EC-G: Shared Household Budget Attribution Prompt

When a user adds an item in a shared inventory, the system must prompt "Who bought this?" with the current user as default. If the user selects another household member, the system must require confirmation. Cross-user inventory merges are permitted for quantity counts, but each spend event must retain its original buyer attribution.

IR-EC-H: Undo for Auto-Merge Actions

Every auto-merge of inventory quantities must be reversible within 10 seconds via an undo control. Undo must revert quantity changes and record the undo in the audit log.

IR-EC-I: Audit Trail for Edge-Case Resolutions

Every edge-case resolution that modifies inventory state must generate a log entry containing at minimum: timestamp, user, household, source, action type, item type reference, item instance reference, spending entry reference, reason, and a reference to the previous state. This log must be accessible to the household admin.

2.2.5. Shared-Household Invitations and Access Changes

In shared households, invitations are the mechanism by which participation is extended, updated, or removed. This section defines the requirement-level behavior needed to support household access changes without introducing implementation-specific onboarding details.

A household invitation is a temporary request that allows a user to join a household context. Each invitation identifies a sender, a recipient, a target household, a status, and an expiration time.

2.2.5.1. Invitation permissions

Invitation permissions determine which household members may invite others into a household. By default, invitation authority belongs to the household admin role, though later requirements may allow additional role-based permissions.

2.2.5.2. Invitation lifecycle

An invitation may move through the following states:

  • Sent — the invitation has been created and is awaiting a response.

  • Accepted — the recipient joins the household and the invitation becomes inactive.

  • Declined — the recipient rejects the invitation and the invitation becomes inactive.

  • Expired — the invitation is no longer valid because its time window has passed.

  • Revoked — the sender or household admin invalidates the invitation before acceptance.

These states matter because they determine whether household participation becomes active and whether access to shared information should be granted or removed.

2.2.5.3. Core rules

  • Invitations to the same recipient for the same household should not remain duplicated in an active state.

  • Expired or revoked invitations cannot be reused.

  • Accepting an invitation grants household access according to the assigned role.

  • Declining or revoking an invitation does not grant access.

  • When a household member is removed, their access to shared household information must no longer remain active.

2.2.5.4. Shared access behavior

Once access is granted, the shared household inventory becomes visible according to the member’s assigned role. Depending on the role definition in later requirements, a member may have view-only access, update privileges, or administrative responsibility.

Household administration may also include viewing current members, resending invitations, changing roles where supported, and revoking access when participation changes.

3.1.1.2. Concept formation: from statements to abstractions

We derive concepts by asking: (1) what phenomena exist in the domain, (2) what information households use to act, and (3) what changes over time.

Examples of abstraction:

  • "If it is not on our fridge note, we assume we are out." → Tracked representation (note/list) vs reality (actual item availability), and assumption under uncertainty.

  • "Rice in two places" → item storage location, and the possibility of multiple locations for the same item.

  • "when it feels light" / "open the backup" → low-stock judgment that is qualitative and strategy-based, and backup as a household practice.

  • "Milk bought twice in the same week" → duplicate purchase as a coordination failure, often arising from missing shared understanding.

  • "3–5 yogurts thrown away last semester" / medicines expired with unopened box → expiration and batches (same item type can exist in multiple packages with different expiration dates).

  • "remove access when someone moves out" → membership and access to shared household information (domain concept: who belongs to the household at a given time).

  • support enforceable permission logic

  • represent domain relationships coherently without unnecessary complexity

User-owned structure

3.1.2.8. Domain Attributes Classification for Core Inventory

This section applies the lecture topic Domain Attributes to the Core Inventory subsystem by classifying key domain entities and behaviors according to their attributes (Static, Dynamic, Dynamic Inert, Dynamic Active, Discrete).

1. Item Type

Represents an item definition (e.g., "Clorox Bleach 64oz Original").

Classification: *Static *Discrete

An item type’s definition does not change over time once created. Its properties (name, barcode, brand, size) remain constant — a "Clorox Bleach 64oz Original" will remain the same regardless of the passage of time, thus it is classified as Static. It is also Discrete because each item type is its own distinct entity in the system.

2. Item Instance (Package)

Represents a specific purchase or concrete unit of an item type, including information such as date of purchase, storage location, and date of expiration if applicable.

Classification: *Dynamic Inert *Discrete

An item instance changes only due to external actions such as household member updates, consumption, or location change. Since it is changed via external stimuli from the household member it is considered Dynamic Inert. Each item instance has a separate record in the system, making it Discrete.

3. Household

Represents a living unit that groups locations and inventory items.

Classification: *Static *Discrete

A household is a structural entity within the system and does not change state over time since it serves as a container for other entities. Each household is unique making it Discrete.

4. Location

Represents a person who belongs to a household and participates in consuming, replenishing, or coordinating around items.

Classification: *Static *Discrete

A household member’s identity does not change over time; while item associations may change, the household member entity itself remains stable so it is Static. Each household member is their own distinct entity, making it Discrete.

6. Duplicate Detection Logic

System logic that determines whether a new entry is a duplicate at either the item type or the item instance level.

Classification: *Dynamic Active

Therefore, the Duplicate Detection Logic is classified as Dynamic Active since it can change via internally programmed rules and various conditions.

3.1.3.4. Conflicts and Tradeoffs

The requirement-level concepts above introduce several tensions that later requirements must resolve explicitly:

  • Speed vs accuracy — requiring too much detail slows capture, but requiring too little may reduce trust in the tracked record.

  • Flexibility vs consistency — household-specific practices are realistic, but too much freedom increases terminology drift and inconsistent usage.

  • Shared visibility vs privacy — shared coordination improves when more information is visible, but some items and spending records remain personal.

  • Timely reminders vs notification fatigue — reminders reduce shortages and waste, but repeated alerts reduce trust and may be disabled.

  • Simplicity vs “smartness” — fixed thresholds and explainable triggers are robust, while predictive or highly adaptive behavior is harder to justify and verify.

  • Automation reliability vs user-device dependence — background checks improve consistency, but they require clear observability, repeatability, and error handling.

These tradeoffs explain why later requirements must emphasize low-friction entry, explainable alerting, deduplication, shared-versus-personal separation, and traceable quantity history.

3.3.5. Data Model vs Transaction Model Conflict

3.3.5.1. Documents Containing Issues

- schema-for-inventory-&-stock-changes.adoc - inventory-transaction-tracking.adoc

3.3.5.2. Problem

The system includes: - tracked inventory records that store current quantity - transaction (quantity change) records that track quantity history

It is unclear whether: - current quantity is stored directly in the tracked inventory record, or - current quantity is derived by applying all transaction (quantity change) records

This creates ambiguity in the data model and may lead to inconsistent implementation, testing, and automation behavior.

3.3.5.3. Resolution

Define the following rule:

Tracked inventory records store the current quantity and are updated whenever a transaction (quantity change) is recorded.

Transaction (quantity change) records serve as append-only historical records that explain how the current quantity was reached over time. They are not the primary source used to compute current quantity during normal operation.

3.3.5.4. Final Decision

Tracked inventory records will store the current quantity directly. Transactions are append-only historical records that explain how the current quantity was reached. When a transaction is recorded, the tracked inventory record quantity is updated immediately.

Current quantity will not be computed by replaying all transactions during normal operation. Transactions are used for history, auditing, and analytics, not for real-time quantity calculation.

When a relevant state transition is detected, the Automation System triggers the Notification Service.

The Notification Service is responsible for creating alert / notification records. The application interface is responsible for displaying in-app notifications. Push delivery, if implemented later, will be handled by a separate Push Service.

This separation ensures clear system boundaries and prevents overlapping responsibilities.

3.4.4. Core Inventory Integrity Concerns Relevant to Verification

This subsection keeps only the integrity concerns that directly affect verification of system behavior. The goal is not to restate a full catalog of runtime field-validation rules, but to identify the constraints that must hold for the tested behaviors to remain meaningful.

The following integrity concerns are relevant to verification:

  • quantities must not become negative

  • item instances must reference valid item types and valid locations

  • expiration dates, when present, must remain logically valid

  • quantity-changing actions must create a corresponding transaction record

  • shared and personal household data must remain distinguishable where required

  • household participation changes must not leave invalid access relationships behind

These concerns matter because incorrect stored data would invalidate later behaviors such as low-stock detection, expiration handling, synchronization, transaction history, and shared-household coordination.

Within this section, these concerns are checked through tests and reviews such as:

  • checking that quantity updates do not produce invalid inventory states

  • checking that expiration handling behaves correctly when dates are present, missing, or unreadable

  • checking that quantity-changing actions preserve append-only transaction history

  • checking that synchronization does not break household, location, or membership relationships

  • checking that shared and personal purchase data remain correctly distinguishable

Detailed field-level constraints and schema-oriented data rules are documented with the corresponding design and data-model material rather than in this validation and verification section.

4.3. Traceability

This section describes how traceability is established in the Digital Home Inventory project. Traceability means our deliverables are linked so we can answer questions about where requirements come from, which personas they serve, and how they connect to domain concepts, scenarios, and verification artifacts.

4.3.1. Overview

Traceability helps us understand relationships between different parts of our documentation. When something changes, we can see what else might be affected. For example, if a requirement is modified, we can identify which personas and needs it supports, which user stories are impacted, and which scenarios/tests should be revisited.

Our approach focuses on creating clear links between artifacts without overcomplicating the documentation (Milestone 1 focuses on progress, not completeness).

4.4. Domain Requirements Acquisition

This section documents how raw domain evidence was gathered and how that evidence was later used during elicitation. To keep these activities distinct, this section separates requirements acquisition from requirements elicitation.

Requirements acquisition focuses on collecting raw domain material before it is interpreted. In this project, acquisition includes questionnaires, short interviews, contextual walkthroughs, and examples of current household tracking practices.

Requirements elicitation begins after acquisition. It includes grouping repeated problems, identifying needs and conflicts, and transforming collected evidence into stories and requirements.

4.4.1. Questionnaire

To support requirements acquisition, the team prepared a short questionnaire aimed at collecting recurring household practices, coordination problems, and tracking pain points before moving into deeper interviews.

The questionnaire focuses on current behavior rather than desired features.

Sample questionnaire items:

* What kinds of household items do you most often lose track of? * How do you currently keep track of what you have at home? * Can you describe a recent time when you bought something you already had? * How do you usually tell that an item is running low? * Do you usually think in exact quantities or rough judgments like “almost empty” or “backup left”? * Do you ever store the same kind of item in multiple places? * Have you ever thrown something away because it expired or was forgotten? * If you live with others, how do you coordinate who buys shared items? * What kinds of reminders would be helpful, and what kinds would feel annoying? * What would make inventory tracking fast enough that you would actually keep using it?

4.4.2. Interview Protocol

The questionnaire is followed by short semi-structured interviews to obtain more specific stories and clarify ambiguous responses.

The protocol used in these sessions is:

#. assign a participant code before taking notes . begin with current tracking habits . ask for one or two recent concrete stories . probe for details such as item, location, what the participant believed, and what actually happened . ask about edge cases such as duplicate purchases, expired items, multiple locations, guests, and move-out situations . summarize the main points back to the participant to confirm understanding

Example prompts include:

* “Tell me about the last time you bought something and later realized you already had it.” * “Walk me through a specific time when you ran out of an item unexpectedly.” * “When you say an item was ‘almost empty’, what made you think that?” * “Can you describe a roommate situation that caused confusion about a shared item?”

4.4.3. Participant Coding

To preserve anonymity while still allowing statements from the same participant to be traced consistently across the document, participants are identified using stable codes.

Examples: * P01 — student living alone * P02 — student in shared apartment * P03 — student in shared apartment * P04 — student athlete * P05 — shared-household organizer

Quotes, notes, and walkthrough findings should refer to participant codes rather than real names.

4.4.4. From Acquisition to Elicitation

After acquisition, raw answers are reviewed and grouped into recurring domain problems and concepts. Those patterns are then used during elicitation to derive needs, stories, and requirements.

The transformation process used in this project is:

#. collect raw questionnaire and interview evidence . group repeated observations and pain points . identify the related domain concept or conflict . derive needs, candidate requirements, or open questions . link those results back to the source evidence

4.4.5. Acquisition Plan

This section defines how raw domain knowledge and evidence are gathered before they are interpreted into stakeholder needs and system requirements.

Requirements acquisition focuses on collecting raw observations, examples, and participant accounts. Elicitation happens later, when that material is interpreted into concepts, stories, and requirements.

4.4.5.1. Goals

  • Capture real household practices instead of idealized descriptions.

  • Understand shared-household coordination, including visibility, duplicates, ownership, and cost splitting.

  • Identify friction points that cause tracking methods to be abandoned.

  • Clarify important edge cases such as bulk purchases, multiple locations, and missing expiration information.

  • Build a traceable base of raw domain evidence for later elicitation.

4.4.5.2. Methods

The following methods are part of requirements acquisition because they gather raw domain data before formal analysis or requirement extraction takes place:

  • Questionnaire: short set of questions used to collect recurring household practices and pain points across participants.

  • Interviews (4–6): semi-structured interviews with solo students and students in shared households, including at least one student athlete.

  • Contextual walkthroughs: participants demonstrate their current tracking methods and explain their shorthand and terminology.

  • Item analysis: review of receipts, pantry or fridge photos, and shared shopping messages to understand how items are tracked or forgotten in practice.

  • Light competitive scan: review a small set of existing apps to identify repeated friction points such as entry effort and notification fatigue.

Note

Broader surveys and larger quantitative validation may be deferred if time is limited. This milestone prioritizes qualitative evidence that supports domain understanding and early requirements work.

4.4.5.4. Risks and Mitigations

  • Risk: Participants are too busy for long interviews.

    • Mitigation: Keep sessions short and allow asynchronous follow-up.

  • Risk: Participants describe ideal behavior instead of actual behavior.

    • Mitigation: Ask for recent concrete examples and current artifacts such as notes, chats, or receipts.

  • Risk: Manual-entry tolerance is very low.

    • Mitigation: Ask explicitly what minimum information participants are willing to record.

  • Risk: Acquisition and elicitation are mixed together too early.

    • Mitigation: Record raw evidence first, then interpret it later in a separate elicitation step.

4.5. Inconsistency Management

An exploration of our domain terminology, database schema, events, actions and behaviors, shared inventory definition, and duplicate item criteria led to the discovery of two inconsistencies. * Event naming clash — "An Inventory has been generated": This event, triggered by the addition of an item to the inventory for the first time, was described as changing the inventory so that it now reflects the presence of a new item. However, the term Inventory is defined in our terminology as "the list of available products in a home," while Item (a synonym for Stock) is defined as "an instance of a product." The event description created a designation clash with the existing glossary: the name alluded to the creation of an inventory, but the described change was a modification of an already-existing inventory. To resolve this clash, the event was split into two: The "What changed" description of "An Inventory has been generated" was updated to: "The inventory is created and reflects the presence of the new item." This separates the creation of the inventory from the modification of an existing inventory, and clarifies that the inventory is created as a direct result of adding an item for the first time. A new event, "A new Product has been generated", was introduced for cases where an item is added to the inventory with no relation to an existing product. This event records the change to the inventory reflecting the new product description as well as the item count and description.

  • Ownership multiplicity mismatch — Group-Owned Inventory model: The Group-Owned Inventory model in the Shared Inventory Definition document describes how all items can be mapped as belonging to the household, with multiple members able to add, edit, and delete them. It further describes that inventories have multiple stock entries, and that stock entries may optionally carry linked owner tags. This implies that items can have multiple owners. However, the ER and table diagrams for database item representation did not reflect this: the model was only satisfiable when items had exactly one owner.

    • To resolve this weak conflict, the ER and table diagrams for database item representation were updated. The relationship between items and owners was changed to a many-to-many relationship with total participation on both sides. A new table, Owns, was added to represent this relationship. This change allows an item to be owned by multiple members while preserving the ability of a member to own multiple items.

Generative AI Disclosure

In our Digital Home Inventory project, we utilized generative AI tools to enhance our workflow while maintaining ownership of the core ideas and decisions. The tools we used were Claude 4.5 (accessed via a free account), ChatGPT (free tier), and DeepSeek AI. These were primarily employed during the brainstorming and structuring phases, where they helped generate initial ideas for project features, such as inventory tracking, notifications for low stock or expirations, budget management, roommate sharing to avoid duplicates, and report generation. For documentation, we used AI to check grammar, suggest edits, and refine the structure to ensure it correlated correctly with rubric criteria, ensuring the documentation met the assignment criteria. The core work originated from our team, starting with original components like our feature analysis, research on requirements (e.g., Supabase schema for items with privacy fields, Flutter UI for scanning), and project scope discussions. AI was used to enhance these, providing suggestions on implementation steps or alternative structures, but we always edited and revised the output to align with our vision. For instance, when AI suggested feature requirements like API integrations for expiration dates, we reviewed them against the A3 document and team discussions, rejecting or modifying those that didn’t fit our student-focused goals. To verify AI-generated content, we fact-checked everything with the team, cross-referencing course materials, rubrics, and real-world examples from similar apps. If inconsistencies arose, such as AI recommendations that conflicted with our privacy feature for personal items, we resolved them through brainstorming sessions, deciding collectively whether to incorporate, edit, or discard. One example: AI proposed a complex budgeting algorithm, but it didn’t align with our simple student needs, so we simplified it after team review. From using AI, we learned it streamlined research and idea generation, freeing up time for deeper reflection on what the app truly needed for student users. It didn’t reduce engagement, instead it forced us to critically evaluate outputs, enhancing our learning and analytical skills. Overall, AI acted as a supportive tool, not a replacement, helping us iterate faster while keeping the project authentic to our group’s efforts.
