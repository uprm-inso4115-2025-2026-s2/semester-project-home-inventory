== Acquisition Plan

This section defines how raw domain knowledge and evidence are gathered before they are interpreted into stakeholder needs and system requirements.

Requirements acquisition focuses on collecting raw observations, examples, and participant accounts. Elicitation happens later, when that material is interpreted into concepts, stories, and requirements.

=== Goals
* Capture real household practices instead of idealized descriptions.
* Understand shared-household coordination, including visibility, duplicates, ownership, and cost splitting.
* Identify friction points that cause tracking methods to be abandoned.
* Clarify important edge cases such as bulk purchases, multiple locations, and missing expiration information.
* Build a traceable base of raw domain evidence for later elicitation.

=== Methods

The following methods are part of requirements acquisition because they gather raw domain data before formal analysis or requirement extraction takes place:

* *Questionnaire:* short set of questions used to collect recurring household practices and pain points across participants.
* *Interviews (4–6):* semi-structured interviews with solo students and students in shared households, including at least one student athlete.
* *Contextual walkthroughs:* participants demonstrate their current tracking methods and explain their shorthand and terminology.
* *Item analysis:* review of receipts, pantry or fridge photos, and shared shopping messages to understand how items are tracked or forgotten in practice.
* *Light competitive scan:* review a small set of existing apps to identify repeated friction points such as entry effort and notification fatigue.

[NOTE]
====
Broader surveys and larger quantitative validation may be deferred if time is limited. This milestone prioritizes qualitative evidence that supports domain understanding and early requirements work.
====

=== Risks and Mitigations
* *Risk:* Participants are too busy for long interviews.
** *Mitigation:* Keep sessions short and allow asynchronous follow-up.

* *Risk:* Participants describe ideal behavior instead of actual behavior.
** *Mitigation:* Ask for recent concrete examples and current artifacts such as notes, chats, or receipts.

* *Risk:* Manual-entry tolerance is very low.
** *Mitigation:* Ask explicitly what minimum information participants are willing to record.

* *Risk:* Acquisition and elicitation are mixed together too early.
** *Mitigation:* Record raw evidence first, then interpret it later in a separate elicitation step.

== Second-Round Requirements Acquisition

This section documents the second round of requirements acquisition conducted. This round focused on gaps and ambiguities found during implementation. The goal is clarify implementation-driven uncertainties around shared-household visibility, role-based permissions, budget alerts, search/filter behavior, barcode fallback, notification fatigue, grocery-list automation, and offline synchronization.

This round simulated short follow-up interviews and lightweight walktroughs with ChatGPT acting as a participant. These simulated participants were shown current app behaviors and asked target questions about ambiguous cases discovered during implementation.

=== Acquisition Q&A Table

[cols="1,1,3,4,4,3"]
|===
| Q&A ID | Priority | Question asked | Answer / evidence summary | Outcome | Requirements added or revised

| M3-QA-01
| High
| When an item is marked personal, what should other household members see?
| M3-P02 and M3-P04 stated that personal items should not appear in the normal shared household inventory by default. M3-P03 said household managers may need to know that storage space is occupied, but not necessarily see payer, cost, notes, or consumption details. The common expectation was: shared items should be visible to the household; personal items should be visible only to the owner unless explicitly shared.
| Personal item visibility must default to private. Shared inventory coordination should not expose personal item details unnecessarily.
| DR-18, IR-09, MR-05-R

| M3-QA-02
| High
| What should viewer, member, and admin roles be allowed to do?
| M3-P03 expected admins to invite/remove members, change roles, and resolve household-level items. M3-P05 expected viewers to see shared needs but not edit quantities. M3-P02 expected normal members to update shared items but not remove other members or change personal items owned by someone else.
| Role behavior must be explicit enough to support implementation and testing.
| DR-19, IR-09, MR-05-R

| M3-QA-03
| Medium
| Which filters are essential when checking inventory at the store?
| M3-P02 prioritized search by item name, shared/personal ownership, and low/out-of-stock status. M3-P03 prioritized location and category. M3-P01 wanted “expiring soon” visible without searching. Participants treated owner/location/status filters as more important than advanced date-range search.
| Inventory lookup must support the minimum filters needed for shopping and planning: name, location, category, ownership/scope, and stock/expiration status.
| DR-20, IR-10, US-RPT-01-R

| M3-QA-04
| High
| When barcode lookup fails, what is the minimum acceptable manual-entry flow?
| M3-P01 and M3-P04 said they would abandon the workflow if too many fields were required. The minimum acceptable fields were item name and quantity/availability. Location and expiration date were useful but should be optional or editable later. M3-P03 wanted the app to let users save quickly and complete details afterward.
| Barcode fallback must not block item capture. Manual entry must allow a minimum viable item record, with richer metadata added later.
| DR-21, IR-11, US-INV-01-R, MR-08-R

| M3-QA-05
| Medium
| Do users want budget alerts, and how should they be delivered?
| M3-P02 wanted warnings before exceeding a weekly grocery budget, especially around 80% and 100%. M3-P03 preferred dashboard/in-app indicators over push notifications for budget warnings. Participants agreed budget alerts should be opt-in because not every household tracks spending strictly.
| Budget alerts should be optional, threshold-based, and preferably in-app by default, with push notifications enabled only by user preference.
| DR-22, IR-12, US-SB-01-R, US-RPT-01-R

| M3-QA-06
| High
| When a shared item is low, expired, or out of stock, who should receive the alert?
| M3-P02 and M3-P03 said everyone should be able to see the alert in the app, but push notifications should not go to every member every time. M3-P05 preferred grouped daily summaries for shared-household alerts. Participants rejected repeated alerts for unchanged conditions.
| Shared alerts should create one household-level alert record, visible in-app to household members, with push/digest delivery controlled by preference and deduplication rules.
| DR-14-R, DR-23, IR-13, MR-07-R

| M3-QA-07
| Medium
| Should auto-added grocery-list items be removable or snoozable?
| M3-P03 liked auto-add behavior but wanted a way to remove or snooze items that were not relevant for the next shopping trip. M3-P02 said the system should not immediately re-add an item after a user removes it unless quantity/status changes again.
| Auto-added restock items must remain user-controllable. Removing or snoozing an auto-added item should suppress re-addition until a new qualifying state transition occurs or the snooze expires.
| DR-24, IR-14

| M3-QA-08
| Medium
| Is offline viewing enough, or are offline updates needed?
| M3-P01 and M3-P02 said offline viewing is useful, but users may need to record quick quantity changes while shopping, in a store with poor connection, or immediately after unloading groceries. Participants accepted pending/sync status if the app made it clear which changes were not yet synchronized.
| Offline support should be revised from view-only to queued pending updates for item creation and quantity changes, with clear sync status and conflict handling.
| MR-03-R, DR-25

=== Acquisition Outcone

The acquisition round produced the following requirement decisions:

* Personal items should be private by default unless explicitly shared.
* Household roles must define clear permissions for viewing, editing, inviting, removing, and administering shared inventory.
* Store-use inventory lookup requires practical filters: name, location, category, ownership/scope, stock status, and expiration status.
* Barcode fallback must support fast manual capture with minimal required fields.
* Budget alerts should be opt-in, threshold-based, and preferably in-app by default.
* Shared alerts should be household-level records with configurable delivery, not repeated push notifications to every member.
* Auto-added grocery-list entries must support manual removal or snooze without immediate re-addition.
* Offline inventory support should include pending item creation and quantity updates, not only view-only access.
