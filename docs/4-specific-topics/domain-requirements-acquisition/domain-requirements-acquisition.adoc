== Domain Requirements Acquisition

This section documents how raw domain evidence was gathered and how that evidence was later used during elicitation. To keep these activities distinct, this section separates requirements acquisition from requirements elicitation.

Requirements acquisition focuses on collecting raw domain material before it is interpreted. In this project, acquisition includes questionnaires, short interviews, contextual walkthroughs, and examples of current household tracking practices.

Requirements elicitation begins after acquisition. It includes grouping repeated problems, identifying needs and conflicts, and transforming collected evidence into stories and requirements.

=== Questionnaire

To support requirements acquisition, the team prepared a short questionnaire aimed at collecting recurring household practices, coordination problems, and tracking pain points before moving into deeper interviews.

The questionnaire focuses on current behavior rather than desired features.

Sample questionnaire items:

* What kinds of household items do you most often lose track of?
* How do you currently keep track of what you have at home?
* Can you describe a recent time when you bought something you already had?
* How do you usually tell that an item is running low?
* Do you usually think in exact quantities or rough judgments like “almost empty” or “backup left”?
* Do you ever store the same kind of item in multiple places?
* Have you ever thrown something away because it expired or was forgotten?
* If you live with others, how do you coordinate who buys shared items?
* What kinds of reminders would be helpful, and what kinds would feel annoying?
* What would make inventory tracking fast enough that you would actually keep using it?


=== Interview Protocol

The questionnaire is followed by short semi-structured interviews to obtain more specific stories and clarify ambiguous responses.

The protocol used in these sessions is:

. assign a participant code before taking notes
. begin with current tracking habits
. ask for one or two recent concrete stories
. probe for details such as item, location, what the participant believed, and what actually happened
. ask about edge cases such as duplicate purchases, expired items, multiple locations, guests, and move-out situations
. summarize the main points back to the participant to confirm understanding

Example prompts include:

* “Tell me about the last time you bought something and later realized you already had it.”
* “Walk me through a specific time when you ran out of an item unexpectedly.”
* “When you say an item was ‘almost empty’, what made you think that?”
* “Can you describe a roommate situation that caused confusion about a shared item?”


=== Participant Coding

To preserve anonymity while still allowing statements from the same participant to be traced consistently across the document, participants are identified using stable codes.

Examples:
* P01 — student living alone
* P02 — student in shared apartment
* P03 — student in shared apartment
* P04 — student athlete
* P05 — shared-household organizer

Quotes, notes, and walkthrough findings should refer to participant codes rather than real names.


=== From Acquisition to Elicitation

After acquisition, raw answers are reviewed and grouped into recurring domain problems and concepts. Those patterns are then used during elicitation to derive needs, stories, and requirements.

The transformation process used in this project is:

. collect raw questionnaire and interview evidence
. group repeated observations and pain points
. identify the related domain concept or conflict
. derive needs, candidate requirements, or open questions
. link those results back to the source evidence
