== Foundational Activities for System Development

A successful system-to-be is built on more than just code. The following activities establish the foundation upon which the Digital Home Inventory system will be developed, ensuring it accurately reflects student needs and performs reliably in real-world conditions.

=== Domain and Requirements Engineering

Learning how students actually live comes first. Through conversations and watching how people manage their daily routines—like using shared notes, talking things over with roommates, or simply trying to remember what they need—the project builds a picture of current habits that exist without any special application. Some students live alone, others share space with several people. Some organize carefully, others wing it. This understanding shapes what the application will eventually do: keep track of things by where they belong in the house, show which items are shared and which are personal, notice when food or medicine is about to expire, and help see where money goes over time. The same application must work for all these different situations. Right now, only broad ideas exist about what students might need. Taking time to understand their actual lives before building anything makes sure those ideas turn into something that fits.

=== Documentation and Knowledge Management

As the project grows, decisions about the application should not live only in people's heads or get buried in old messages. Writing things down in one place gives everyone access to the same information. Using simple text files kept alongside the work itself means the documentation stays close to the code it describes. When someone joins later, they can read about why certain choices were made and how different pieces fit together. The same record helps keep everyone using the same words for the same things—whether "pantry" means only food or includes cleaning supplies, for example. Without this habit, people start remembering things differently or making assumptions that others do not share. The project becomes harder to keep consistent as new features get added over time.

=== Quality Assurance Planning

An application that surprises users or fails when they need it will not become part of anyone's routine. Knowing when a feature is truly finished means agreeing on what success looks like beforehand. For alerts, success might mean a student finds out about expiring yogurt in time to eat it. For the shared list, it might mean roommates see the same information when deciding who goes to the store. Walking through made-up but realistic situations with actual students helps catch mismatches between what the team builds and what users actually need. Talking through examples together—like a week in the life of someone trying to stick to a grocery budget—shows whether the planned features will hold up in real life. The point is an application that students find genuinely helpful, not one that simply passes technical checks.

=== Project Management and Coordination

Several groups building different pieces of the same application need to stay connected. Regular conversations between teams working on inventory, alerts, budgeting, and reports help spot potential trouble before it slows everyone down. A shared board shows who is working on what and what still needs attention. Looking over each other's work before it becomes part of the main application catches small issues early. When the team working on reports needs information about spending, checking in with the budgeting team early saves redoing work later. These habits of talking and coordinating mean that when it comes time to bring everything together, the pieces fit without surprises.
